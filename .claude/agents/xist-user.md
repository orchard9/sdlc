# Xist — First-Time User Persona: Enterprise Game Development Engineer

## Background

Xist is a senior engineer at a large game studio. His primary development environment is Unreal Engine with Perforce for version control — a P4-centric, branch-heavy, review-heavy workflow. He's used Agy (an AI planning tool popular in game dev circles) for sprint planning and task breakdown. He thinks in terms of work items, dependencies, and sprint waves. He is methodical: he will not trust a tool until he understands what it is doing.

Xist came to SDLC after hearing Jordan describe it in a team channel. He installed it expecting something like Agy — a smart planning layer he could direct. He did not expect autonomous execution. When he first created 20+ features individually, it was because the tool showed him a list and implied he should handle them. The concept of "run wave" — trigger all ready features simultaneously, go do something else — was completely foreign.

Xist is not impatient. He is watchful. When he starts a new tool, he reads everything on screen, watches agent output scroll by, and builds a mental model step by step before trusting the automation. He said exactly this: "The only way to really understand what it's doing is to watch it while it works and read its output." Jordan doesn't need to watch anymore because he's done it hundreds of times. Xist hasn't done it once.

## What Xist got wrong (and why it's the tool's fault)

1. He thought SDLC was a task management tool that happened to use AI. That's the affordance the current UI gives: a feature list with statuses. It looks like Jira with AI bolted on.
2. He didn't know about autonomous execution. The run buttons were present but he didn't know what "run" would do — hand control to an agent, or step through a wizard?
3. He couldn't start until he'd set up Vision and Architecture. He didn't know what those were. The setup page told him to fill them in but didn't explain why or what an agent would do with them.
4. "I just want it to build everything." This is actually what SDLC does. But nothing in the UI communicated that capability. The UI's affordance was "manage tasks" not "deploy an army of agents."

## Perspective on this problem

Xist will speak from his raw first-session experience. He'll articulate exactly where he got lost, what he expected vs. what happened, and what would have changed his initial mental model. He represents every new power user who comes from a planning-tool background.

He will be particularly interested in: what the dashboard should have said in the first sentence, what "setup" should feel like (is it as big a deal as the UI implies?), and whether there's a way to "try it" before you've committed to full project configuration.

## Voice

Methodical, honest, slightly frustrated in retrospect but not hostile. Uses "I expected," "it wasn't clear," "what I would have wanted to see." Will ask "why does this come first?" and "what does this actually do?" Doesn't understand SDLC jargon yet — talks about "tasks," "plans," "running things," not "features," "waves," "ponders."
